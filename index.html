Abstract

This project investigates how new immigrant families access, understand and participate in local community activities. Although community activities can support social integration and family wellbeing, many newcomer families face barriers such as fragmented activity information, language concerns, unclear activity processes, social uncertainty, and difficulty judging whether an activity is suitable for their children.

Based on early user research, including questionnaires, semi-structured interviews and research notes, we identified key themes through affinity mapping and prioritised design opportunities using How Might We questions. In response, we designed Roota, a high-fidelity Figma prototype of a community activity app for new immigrant families. Roota supports activity browsing, practical activity details, scenario simulation, Friends Participate prompts and a child-friendly Kids Mode.

The prototype was evaluated through formative usability testing using Think Aloud, semi-structured interviews and behavioural observation. The findings show that users could understand the basic activity information but needed more practical details such as check-in time, parking, navigation and what to bring. Scenario simulation helped users imagine the activity process and reduce uncertainty; Friends Participate provided social reassurance for some users and Kids Mode was visually engaging but required age-appropriate content and parental controls.

Overview

A platform for the moment before joining in

Roota helps newly arrived families discover local community activities, understand what to expect and feel confident enough to actually turn up — moving past scattered listings to real participation.

In a multicultural society, join community activities supports belonging and family wellbeing. However, for newcomer families the hardest part isn't a lack of information — it's everything around the information. Not know what an activity involves, whether it suits their child or how to handle the language or whether they'll know anyone there.

Across 10 interviews and 31 survey responses, then a 6 participant usability evaluation. This project traces the journey from knowing about an activity to decide to join it and designs for the uncertainty in between.

The problem

Information is everywhere — participation isn't

Existing literature suggests that simply presenting information is not enough to drive participation. Language confidence, social trust and understanding the activity itself all shape the decision to join.

For families with young children, children can mediate connections between the family and the wider community (Katz, 2014). However, parents may still struggle to move beyond informal networks and build a sense of belonging.

Language confidence also shapes how newcomers access information and take part in community life (Kuperman, 2022). At the same time, families often rely on trusted contacts and “arrival brokers” rather than official channels when navigating unfamiliar systems (Wessendorf, 2022; Hans, 2023).

So newcomer families don't face a single barrier. They face a connected chain: fragmented information → uncertainty about whether to go → language and social pressure → reliance on familiar faces. Even when information exists, it's hard to act on in an unfamiliar system (Bahl et al., 2025) — each link makes the next decision harder.

Ideally, there would be an app that could integrate the activities, or clearly tell me what I need to do.

— Survey respondent, open-ended response

Landscape

What today's platforms miss

We learned about different information channels. Each holds part of the picture — but none support the decision to participate.

What's On (Melbourne)Gov platform
+Integrates most local activities; authoritative.
–No schedule or “what to prepare”; not newcomer-friendly; no parent reviews or atmosphere.
Eventbrite · MeetupEvent platform
+Clear categories; online registration & payment.
–Few activities for children; assumes local cultural background; feels like a ticketing app.
Community CentresLocal org sites
+Basic info with age ranges and brief descriptions.
–Unclear procedures; little decision support; no “is it beginner-friendly?” signal.
Library programsCouncil libraries
+Clear, simple language; family-friendly; well-defined types.
–No first-timer guidance, social context, recommendations or language support.
The gap

Existing platforms are built to present information. None help a newcomer family judge whether an activity suits them, picture what will happen or feel socially safe enough to go. Roota designs for that missing layer — decision support, preview and trust.

Define · Key themes

Four barriers, one chain

Thematic analysis distilled the research into four themes that build on each other — from find information all the way to feel safe enough to show up.

THEME 01

Fragmented information access

Activity information is scattered across schools, friends, WeChat groups, social media and official sites. Families rely on informal channels and rarely use official platforms, so many activities stay unknown.

“We won't specifically look for these activities.”P2 · INTERVIEW
67%get information through friends — only 20% use government sites
THEME 02

Uncertainty in deciding to participate

Even when information exists, parents can't easily judge an activity's process, content or suitability for their child. Descriptions are too brief to support a confident decision.

“If I don't understand the specific process, I may hesitate.”P1 · INTERVIEW
30%name unclear process information as a top barrier
THEME 03

Language & social barriers

Limited language confidence becomes social anxiety. Families fear miscommunication and awkwardness from cultural differences — which suppresses actual participation, not just conversation.

“When facing others, I can't speak up and feel very nervous.”P5 · INTERVIEW
53%say language is the main barrier to taking part
THEME 04

Reliance on familiar social networks

Families are far more willing to attend when someone they know is going. Acquaintances provide “social safety” and lower the threshold — but can also limit access to the wider community.

“If I have someone I know with me, I'd be more willing to go.”P5 · INTERVIEW
33%feel unfamiliar with the local community

Deliver · High fidelity

The Roota prototype

A high-fidelity prototype designed to help newcomer families understand, evaluate and feel prepared for local community activities. Roota built in Figma and combines clear activity details, suitability tags, scenario previews, social cues and a child-friendly mode into one guided experience.

A five-minute walkthrough of the prototype and research.

A short setup captures language, suburb, family make-up, children's ages and interests — so recommendations feel personal from the very first screen.

On the first visit, gentle coachmarks walk new families through the home feed, the suitability tags and Child Mode — so nothing feels unfamiliar on day one.

The core home — a centralised feed of nearby activities with a “For you” recommendation, category filters and clear suitability tags. A translate toggle switches the whole app into the family's language and Child Mode turns the home playful. The activity detail lays out a what-to-expect timeline before you decide.

Seeing familiar faces lowers the barrier to join. Families see who's going — people they know plus other families with language and dietary tags — then tap Check In on arrival for social reassurance.

An illustrated map of your city. Activities appear as pins you can tap and check in lights up new neighbourhoods — turn the exploration into a gentle game.

Light gamification rewards show up — earn points for attending and redeem them for real rewards. Climb a friendly leaderboard and collect badges that light up across the map.

A family's home base — the profile gathers points earned, friends, leaderboard rank and badges in one place, with full settings for account, notifications, privacy and support.

All participants completed every task — the core path is clear. The depth of information and interaction is where the work remains.

✓ What worked
  • Basic activity information was understandable; colour-coded tags helped families judge suitability fast.
  • Scenario simulation reduced unfamiliarity — “It can greatly reduce my fear of this activity.”
  • The simulation felt playful — described as “like reading a comic” and “like a mini-game.”
  • Friends Participate created social safety — an acquaintance “lowers my vigilance a little.”
  • Kids Mode's picture-book style was appealing and family-friendly.
→ What to improve
  • Activity details lacked logistics: check-in time, full address, parking, transport and what to bring.
  • Simulation needed more steps and branches.
  • Kids Mode needs age-tiered content (3–6 / 7–9 / 10–12) and a child lock.
  • Friends Participate needs clearer privacy control — who can see my participation status?
  • Some labels (“Practice here”, “Let's go”, “Check in”) were ambiguous for newcomers.

Recommendations carried into the next iteration

Make discovery obvious

Promote “See all”, add more activities and stronger personalisation on the home feed.

Add practical logistics

Expand the timeline with arrival time, parking, transport and a what-to-bring section.

Deepen the simulation

Branch outcomes, add common social moments.

Give privacy control

Let users choose who sees their status.

Age-tier Kids Mode

Offer 3–6 / 7–9 / 10–12 versions, a “Tell my parent” path and a child lock.

Keep rewards gentle

Use badges as encouragement for milestones — never let the app become reward-driven.

10

Reflection

More than a listings app

Newcomer families need more than activity listings — they need to understand what to expect before deciding to participate.

The features that resonated weren't about more information — they were about reduce uncertainty. Rehearse a scenario, see a friend attend or preview the day as a story. That's where Roota earned trust.

The clearest next step is to pair that emotional reassurance with the practical logistics families asked for — arrival times, parking, navigation and what to bring. Then deepen the simulation so choices genuinely shape the outcome. Future testing should also include more diverse families and ethically, direct feedback from children.
